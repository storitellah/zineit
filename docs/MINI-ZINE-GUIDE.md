# Making a 16-page mini zine

Four sheets of A4. Printed both sides, folded together, stapled through the spine.
This is the same construction a print shop calls *saddle stitch* — the difference is
you can do it at a kitchen table for the price of the paper.

## Before you lay anything out

A 16-page saddle-stitched zine has **16 pages, covers included**:

| Page | What it is |
|---|---|
| 1 | Front cover |
| 2 | Inside front cover |
| 3–14 | The zine |
| 15 | Inside back cover |
| 16 | Back cover |

Page counts must be a multiple of four — that is arithmetic, not a preference. Each
folded sheet contributes four pages, so there is no such thing as an 18-page saddle
zine. ZineIt fixes the count at 16 for this format so you cannot back yourself into
a corner halfway through.

## Reading order is not print order

The single hardest thing about a folded zine is that the printer never sees your
pages in order. Fold a sheet in half and page 1 ends up back-to-back with page 2 but
side-by-side with page 16.

ZineIt works this out for you. Open **Print 16-page sheets (saddle stitch)** and you
get both views:

```
Sheet 1  front   16 |  1        ← the covers
Sheet 1  back     2 | 15
Sheet 2  front   14 |  3
Sheet 2  back     4 | 13
Sheet 3  front   12 |  5
Sheet 3  back     6 | 11
Sheet 4  front   10 |  7
Sheet 4  back     8 |  9        ← the centre spread
```

Two things worth noticing, because they tell you the imposition is right:

- **Every sheet-side pairs to 17.** 16+1, 2+15, 14+3 … that sum (pages + 1) is the
  defining property of saddle stitch. If a pair doesn't sum to 17, something is wrong.
- **Pages 8 and 9 sit on the innermost sheet.** They are the only true facing pair in
  the whole zine — the only spread that opens flat with no fold between the images.
  If you have one photograph that deserves to run across two pages, put it there.

## Printing

1. **Paper**: A4, landscape.
2. **Scale**: 100%. Not "fit to page" — that shrinks your trim size and your bleed
   stops working.
3. **Margins**: none.
4. **Double-sided, flipped on the SHORT edge.**

That last one is the mistake everyone makes once. Flip on the long edge and every
second page comes out upside down. If your printer driver says "flip on short edge"
or shows a little book icon versus a notepad icon, you want the notepad.

**Print one on plain paper first and fold it.** A test copy costs a single sheet and
catches page-order mistakes, upside-down backs, and text drowning in the gutter. It
is the cheapest insurance in printmaking.

## Folding

Fold each sheet in half, printed side out, and crease hard. A bone folder is ideal;
the back of a spoon works; your thumbnail leaves marks.

Then **nest** the sheets inside one another: sheet 1 outermost (it carries the
covers), sheet 4 innermost (it carries pages 8–9). Nesting, not stacking — this is
what makes the page order come out right.

## Binding

**Staples.** Two, through the spine fold, about a quarter of the way in from the top
and bottom. A long-arm or saddle stapler reaches the centre easily. Without one: open
a standard stapler flat, lay the zine over a soft eraser, staple down into the eraser,
then turn it over and close the legs with your thumbnail or a spoon.

**Thread.** A pamphlet stitch looks better and costs nothing but time. Pierce three
or five evenly spaced holes along the fold with an awl. Sew from the inside centre
out, along the fold, and back, ending inside. Tie a square knot around the long
stitch, straddling it, and trim to about a centimetre.

**Accordion or loose cards.** Both fine. Neither needs the imposition above — set
your pages up as single pages and print sequentially instead.

## Trimming

Trim the fore-edge **last**, after binding. As pages nest, the inner ones creep
outward — a thick zine can push 2–3 mm. Trimming after binding squares that off. A
guillotine is fastest; a steel rule and a fresh scalpel blade on a cutting mat give a
cleaner edge than a blunt guillotine ever will.

If you designed with bleed on, your images already run past the trim line, so a
slightly imperfect cut still shows ink rather than a white sliver.

## Checking your work

- Read the test copy cover to cover. Page numbers in order?
- Does anything important sit within 5 mm of the fold? The gutter eats it.
- Hold a spread up to the light — do the images on facing pages align across the fold?
- Is the front cover on the outside of sheet 1? If not, your flip setting is wrong.
